Introduction

Say you are a college student and you moved to a new hostel. You go to the mess to have food, where you find two options you can choose from: rice and roti. In this situation, ponder over the following questions and get answers for them.

  • Which one (between rice and roti) would you pick?
  • Would you trust the decision that you made? Why?
  • Would your decision change when you go to the mess the next day?
  • Based on what do you make your decision?

Please proceed after answering the above questions. It makes a significant difference in how much you would take away from this post!

It's not difficult to realize that eventhough you have certain preferences towards rice/roti based on your experiences in the past, you might make a decision that doesn't adhere to your preferences. Maybe you like roti, but you observe that the rotis in the mess are burnt. Maybe you like rice and you chose to take that, but you didn't like what you ate, which made you pick roti on the next day. What if you didn't like the roti you picked on the next day and decided to go back to rice as your preferred choice instead?

These are the kinds of questions one asks when making decisions. The focus of sequential decision making, however, is to try and understand different kinds of methods you can use to make decisions over time in a certain situation or environment.

Instagram or Harry Potter?

Consider another situation. Everyday you come home after work, have dinner, go to bed at around 10PM and scroll through Instagram reels until you fall asleep. You are frustrated with yourself for being an Instagram addict and decided to start reading books instead. From then on, you have a choice at 10PM - watch Instagram reels or read Harry Potter until you fall asleep. What choice do you pick?

One might think "That's simple, pick Harry Potter! Is this even a decision making problem?", and you might be right. But more often than not, you would be wrong because switching habits forcefully might tempt you to fall back to your old habits soon after! What if you don't enjoy Harry Potter as much as Instagram reels?

Human beings aren't rational beings and their priorities aren't always set in stone. You might feel all frustrated with your behaviour and stay determined to change your habits in the morning, but as soon as you get on the bed you might think "You know what? I'll watch a few reels and then start reading the book". Obviously you never pick up the book, and the next day you regret your decision. But guess what? You repeat the same thing the next day and regret again the day after. The endless cycle of regret eats your mind up. (feeling relatable, aren't we? 😏)

My point is that it's not an obvious choice to make, and the problem mentioned above can be modeled as a sequential decision making problem. You could decide to gradually decrease the amount of time you spend on Instagram over the span of a week and change your habit before you start reading books. This might be helpful considering that habits are, in some sense, sticky (you are more likely to follow a habit today if you followed it yesterday). You might instead decide to forcibly switch habits for a week and trust in yourself that you will stick to your new habit thereafter, which could work if you have plenty of discipline. This sequential decision making problem is highly personal and might differ for different people.

Online Learning can help us mathematically model problems like this and help us make optimal decisions. The habit problem mentioned above isn't a conventional problem in Online Learning literature, in fact it is a problem I would have a lot of fun modeling! I hope that I would be able to model a complex problem such as this eventually, after I learn enough. Why don't you think of a sequential decision making problem that you would like to solve yourself?

There are a certain class of problems called the Bandit problems where the task is to pick the best choice from a given set of choices. Trying to understand how to think of such problems is a good starting point to learn more!

The Bandit Problem

According to ( Citation: Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020, p. 8 Lattimore, T. & Szepesvári, C. (2020). Bandit Algorithms (1). Cambridge University Press. Retrieved from https://www.cambridge.org/core/product/identifier/9781108571401/type/book ) , the term "Bandit" in the bandit problem comes from the two-armed bandit machines, which are slot machines with two arms. You can insert a coin into the slot machine and pull an arm, following which you might get some reward. Different arms can give you different rewards (each arm could have a different reward distribution) and the problem is in deciding which arm to pull. Think about it. If you had a chance to operate a two-armed bandit, which arm would you pull? While this is an interesting question, it might not be so interesting if you're broke. Afterall, the slot machines are called bandits because they steal your money!

Bandit problems were first introduced in the context of medical trials, in order to answer questions like How do you change treatment strategy for patients based on their response to past treatment? Now that we know how useful and important Bandit problems can be, let's look at a trivial example that is easy for my primitive brain to explain.

The ballpoint pen dilemma

I've always had a problem with buying ballpoint pens, in fact I still do. Which pen is the best? The stationery store has a variety of options that I can choose from, and yet I find it difficult to decide which one to buy! I want a pen that works for me, but what does "works" mean? I'm not sure, but my hope is that one day I will find my dream pen. From then on, whenever I go out to buy a pen, I shall not look at any pen other than my dream pen.

This problem can be modeled as a multi armed bandit problem, where each choice of pen is an arm. Say that I decide to choose only between Butterflow and Pin Point, in which case the problem can be modeled as a two-armed bandit problem. Given that I have previously purchased one of these pens each month over the past 8 months, my goal is to make choices over the next 12 months to maximize my future satisfaction. I rated each of my previous experience with the pens (an integer in {1,2,3,4,5}), which is shown in the following table.

Here, I am the learner who must decide which pen to buy next month depending upon my writing experience. All my experience with my pen that includes writing class notes, solving assignments, and making my phone charger fit into plug points is encapsulated in a single term called the environment. On every $t^{th}$ month, I make my purchase decision, or action, denoted by $A_t \in \{0,1\}$ such that $A_t=0$ if I decide to buy Butterflow and $A_t=1$ if I decide to buy Pin Point. At the end of every month, I rate my experience for the $t^{th}$ month, which I call reward and denote that with $X_t \in \{1,2,3,4,5\}$.

I can represent the above table in a single sequence of actions and rewards that I call history, which is given by $H_t = (A_1, X_1, A_2, X_2, ... , A_t, X_t)$. Since I must make a decision based on the history, I can represent my decision making process using a policy, which is a function that maps history to an action. I shall be buying pens for only 12 more months, and I call this time period horizon, denoted by $n$. My goal is to find a policy that maximizes the cumulative reward over the horizon i.e. maximize $\sum_{t=1}^{n} X_t$.

How do I come up with a good policy?

The answer to the above question isn't that simple for multiple reasons.

Firstly, we do not know much about the environment. Simply put, I do not know how I will be using my pen in the future, or how the pen will behave. What if I put too much pressure on the pen and damage it? What if the pen stops working all of a sudden for whatever reason? What if I must give my pen to my trustworthy friend who will render it useless with high probability? Whatever the case is, the overall experience contributes to the final reward given by the environment.

Secondly, say I decided to buy Pin Point and had a very good experience. Now, should I exploit what I learned from my experience and buy Pin Point in the next month too? If there is a chance that I would like Butterflow better, wouldn't I be missing a chance to explore the other option and maximize my reward, if I choose to buy Pin Point? Also, if I decide to exploit, how would I know for sure whether I would like my experience with Pin Point in the next month as well? This is called the exploration-exploitation dilemma, and it plays an important role in all Bandit algorithms.

Regret makes you stronger!

Say I decide to buy different pens every month because I believed that would be the best strategy I could use. However, at the end of 12 months, how would I know that my strategy (policy) was indeed the best strategy (optimal policy)? Since I can't go back in time, I could choose to calculate my regret relative to another policy. For example, I could ask myself "What would be my expected reward if I chose to buy Pin Point every month? Would it be more than the reward I got?". If the answer to my second question is yes, then I have no choice but to curl up on my bed and cry in despair.

That's why, don't postpone regretting until you're done with life. Regret every day, so you have a chance to improve everyday!

If you would take a moment and ignore my unsolicited life advice, you would see that I have a point. At every month, you can subtract the total expected reward of your policy from the total expected reward of another candidate policy and analyse that value, which I call regret. If the regret is positive, then you could switch to the other policy in order to maximize your total expected reward. If not, you can choose to keep using the same policy, or you could also calculate regret relative to another policy.

To find the optimal policy (the policy with the maximum total expected reward), you must compute regret of your policies over all possible policies and find the policy with the maximum regret. However, it's important to note that the expected reward calculations are based on the history and assumptions about the reward distributions, and finding the optimal policy would be hard without these prerequisites. For example, think about this - can you accurately determine which buying strategy is better only after buying a single pen?
